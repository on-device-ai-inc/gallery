## ARTICLE 8: TAX ON SPLIT INCOME (TOSI) COMPLIANCE

### 8.1 TOSI Overview and Trustee Obligations

**Tax on Split Income ("TOSI")** under subsection 120.4 of the Income Tax Act (Canada) applies highest marginal tax rate (~53.53%) to certain income received by individuals under age 25 from private corporations or trusts, UNLESS specific exceptions apply.

**Trustees must comply with TOSI rules when distributing income to Beneficiaries under age 25.**

### 8.2 TOSI Application by Age

**(a) Under Age 18:**

Income distributed to Beneficiaries under age 18 is subject to TOSI UNLESS:
- Income is from property inherited from parent (not applicable here)
- Income is reasonable return on capital contributed by Beneficiary (unlikely for minors)
- Other narrow exceptions (generally not applicable)

**Practical impact:** Very limited income distributions to minor Beneficiaries.

**(b) Age 18-24:**

Income distributed to Beneficiaries age 18-24 is subject to TOSI UNLESS one of the following exceptions applies:

**Exception 1 - Excluded Business:**
- Beneficiary worked ≥20 hours/week in Corporation during the taxation year (averaged over 12 months), OR
- Beneficiary worked ≥20 hours/week in Corporation in any 5 prior taxation years (can be non-consecutive)
- If exception met, ALL income from Corporation is "excluded business" and TOSI doesn't apply

**Exception 2 - Reasonable Return:**
- Income represents reasonable return for labor contributed to Corporation, OR
- Income represents reasonable return for capital contributed to Corporation

**Exception 3 - Arm's Length:**
- Income is from arm's length source (not applicable for family trust/corporation)

**(c) Age 25 and Over:**

TOSI generally does NOT apply to individuals age 25+ who own ≥10% of Corporation (by votes or value).

**For this Trust:** Once Beneficiaries reach age 25, distributions are generally TOSI-exempt.

### 8.3 Excluded Business Exception - Labor Contribution

**The most practical TOSI exception is the "excluded business" exception based on labor contribution.**

**Requirements:**
- Beneficiary works ≥20 hours/week (averaged) in Corporation
- Can be measured over the current taxation year, OR
- Can be measured over any 5 prior taxation years (total, not consecutive)

**Examples:**

**Example 1 - Current Year Labor:**
- Child 1 (age 22) works full-time (40 hrs/week) in Corporation during summer (May-August = 16 weeks)
- Works part-time (10 hrs/week) during school year (September-April = 32 weeks)
- Total hours: (16 weeks × 40 hrs) + (32 weeks × 10 hrs) = 960 hours
- Average: 960 hours ÷ 52 weeks = 18.5 hrs/week
- **DOES NOT meet 20 hr/week threshold - TOSI applies**

**Example 2 - Multi-Year Labor:**
- Child 2 (age 23) worked in Corporation:
  - Age 18: 15 hrs/week averaged
  - Age 19: 25 hrs/week averaged ✓
  - Age 20: 18 hrs/week averaged
  - Age 21: 22 hrs/week averaged ✓
  - Age 22: 0 hrs/week (studied abroad)
- Met ≥20 hrs/week in 2 of 5 prior years
- **DOES NOT meet "5 years" requirement - TOSI applies**

**Example 3 - Meets Exception:**
- Child 3 (age 24) worked in Corporation:
  - Age 17: 25 hrs/week (summer job)
  - Age 18: 22 hrs/week
  - Age 19: 21 hrs/week
  - Age 20: 30 hrs/week (gap year before university)
  - Age 21: 20 hrs/week (co-op placement)
- Met ≥20 hrs/week in 5 of past 7 years
- **MEETS exception - TOSI does NOT apply**

### 8.4 Documentation Requirements

**To claim excluded business exception, Trustees must maintain:**

**(a) Timesheets or Work Logs:**
- Hours worked per week (or per pay period)
- Dates worked
- Signed by Beneficiary and supervisor (if applicable)

**(b) Job Description:**
- Role and responsibilities
- Reporting structure
- Deliverables or performance metrics

**(c) Compensation Records:**
- Salary or wages paid (if any)
- T4 slips issued
- Comparison to market rates for similar roles (demonstrates reasonableness)

**(d) Evidence of Genuine Work:**
- Emails, project deliverables, code commits, design work
- Contemporaneous documentation (not recreated later)
- Supervisor or customer confirmation of work performed

### 8.5 Trustee Strategy for TOSI Compliance

The Trustees shall implement the following strategy to enable TOSI-exempt distributions:

## ARTICLE 9: SECTION 107(2) ROLLOVER AND DISTRIBUTION MECHANICS

### 9.1 Section 107(2) Overview

**Subsection 107(2) of the Income Tax Act** provides tax-deferred rollover treatment when Trust distributes capital property (such as Corporation shares) to Canadian resident Beneficiaries.

**Tax treatment:**
- Trust deems disposition at adjusted cost base (ACB), not fair market value (FMV)
- No capital gains tax triggered on distribution
- Beneficiary receives property at Trust's ACB (tax cost rolled over)
- Beneficiary's holding period includes Trust's holding period for LCGE purposes

**Significance:** Enables Trustees to distribute Corporation shares to Beneficiaries without triggering immediate capital gains tax, allowing Beneficiaries to claim individual LCGE on subsequent sale.

### 9.2 Requirements for Section 107(2) Rollover

Section 107(2) rollover treatment applies ONLY if ALL of the following requirements are met:

**(a) Capital Property:**
Property distributed must be capital property (shares qualify).

**(b) Canadian Resident Beneficiary:**
Beneficiary must be resident in Canada immediately before distribution.

**(c) No Subsection 75(2):**
Subsection 75(2) must NOT apply to the Trust (this Trust is structured to avoid subsection 75(2) - see Articles 2.3, 3.2, 3.4, 3.5).

**(d) No Section 107(4.1) Taint:**
Property must not have been acquired by Trust on a tax-deferred rollover from a non-resident or in certain other tainted circumstances (not applicable here).

**(e) Election Filed (if Required):**
Trust and Beneficiary may need to jointly elect Section 107(2) rollover treatment (CPA to advise on filing requirements).

### 9.3 Optimal Distribution Timing

**(a) Before Anticipated Exit (LCGE Maximization):**

If Corporation exit is anticipated within 2-4 years, Trustees should consider distributing shares to Beneficiaries at least **24 months before anticipated exit**, so that:
- Each Beneficiary holds shares directly for 24 months
- Each Beneficiary qualifies for LCGE on exit
- Family LCGE shelter maximized

**Example Timeline:**
- **March 2026:** Trust receives 400 Class B shares via estate freeze
- **March 2028:** Exit anticipated in 24 months (March 2030)
- **March 2028:** Trustees distribute shares to 4 Beneficiaries (100 shares each via Section 107(2))
- **March 2030:** Corporation sold for $10M
- **Result:** Each Beneficiary holds shares for 24 months, qualifies for LCGE, claims $1.254M exemption

**(b) Years 19-20 of Trust (Deemed Disposition Avoidance):**

If no exit is anticipated before Year 21, Trustees should distribute shares in **Years 19-20** (2044-2046) to avoid 21-year deemed disposition:

**21-Year Deemed Disposition (Subsection 104(4)):**
- On Trust's 21st anniversary (2047), Trust deemed to dispose of all capital property at FMV
- Capital gains tax triggered (no rollover)
- Tax cost: potentially $millions if Corporation has appreciated significantly

**Avoidance Strategy:**
- Distribute shares via Section 107(2) rollover in 2044-2045 (Years 19-20)
- Beneficiaries receive shares at Trust's ACB (no tax on distribution)
- 21-year deemed disposition doesn't occur (shares no longer held by Trust)
- Beneficiaries hold shares directly until eventual exit

### 9.4 Distribution Mechanics

When Trustees distribute Corporation shares to Beneficiaries via Section 107(2) rollover:

**Step 1: Trustee Resolution**
Trustees pass unanimous resolution authorizing distribution, specifying:
- Date of distribution
- Shares to be distributed (e.g., 100 Class B shares to each of 4 Beneficiaries)
- Section 107(2) rollover election
- Tax cost (ACB) to be transferred

**Step 2: CPA Prepares Election (if required)**
CPA prepares Section 107(2) election forms (if required under Tax Act).

**Step 3: Share Transfer**
Trustees transfer shares from Trust to Beneficiaries:
- Endorsed share certificates, OR
- Director's resolution approving transfer, OR
- Securities transfer form (if shares dematerialized)

**Step 4: Corporation Updates Share Register**
Corporation updates shareholder register to reflect Beneficiaries as new shareholders.

**Step 5: Tax Reporting**
Trust reports distribution on T3 return:
- Deemed disposition at ACB (no capital gain)
- Distribution to Beneficiaries noted
- Beneficiaries receive T3 slips showing tax cost

**Step 6: Beneficiaries File Tax Returns**
Beneficiaries report receipt of shares:
- ACB equals Trust's ACB (rollover treatment)
- No immediate tax
- Holding period for LCGE purposes includes Trust's holding period

### 9.5 Holding Period for LCGE

**CRITICAL TAX RULE:**

To claim LCGE, Beneficiary must hold QSBC shares for at least **24 months** before disposition.

**When does 24-month holding period start?**

**If shares distributed via Section 107(2) rollover:**
- Beneficiary's holding period INCLUDES the Trust's holding period
- Trust acquired shares in March 2026 via estate freeze
- Trust distributes shares to Beneficiaries in March 2028
- Beneficiaries deemed to have held shares since March 2026 (Trust's acquisition date)
- **Beneficiaries meet 24-month test immediately upon distribution**

**Example:**
- Trust acquired shares: March 1, 2026
- Trust distributed shares to Beneficiaries: March 1, 2028 (24 months later)
- Corporation sold: April 1, 2028 (1 month after distribution)
- **Result:** Beneficiaries meet 24-month holding period (March 2026 to April 2028 = 25 months)

**This is a KEY advantage of Section 107(2) rollover - Beneficiaries inherit Trust's holding period.**

### 9.6 Distribution to Non-Resident Beneficiaries

**If a Beneficiary is NOT resident in Canada at time of distribution:**
- Section 107(2) rollover does NOT apply
- Distribution is deemed disposition at FMV (capital gains tax triggered)
- Withholding tax may apply
- Beneficiary receives shares at FMV tax cost

**Trustees should:**
- Confirm Beneficiaries' residency status before distribution
- Defer distribution if Beneficiary temporarily abroad (studying, working)
- Consult with tax advisor regarding non-resident Beneficiary planning

**(a) At Time of Sale (Disposition Test):**
- ≥90% of Corporation's assets must be used in active business carried on primarily in Canada

**(c) Shareholding Test:**

- Accounts receivable (trade receivables from customers)
- Intellectual property used in business
- Office space, furniture
- Software, technology
- Cash required for working capital (≤3-6 months operating expenses)

**Passive assets:**
- Excess cash (>6 months operating expenses)
- Marketable securities (stocks, bonds, mutual funds)
- Real estate held for investment (not business use)
- Loans to shareholders or related parties
- Investments in other companies

**Quarterly Monitoring Protocol:**

Trustees (in coordination with Corporation and CPA) shall monitor quarterly:
- Total asset FMV
- Active asset FMV
- Passive asset FMV
- Active % = Active FMV ÷ Total FMV

**Monitoring Schedule:**

| Quarter End | Active % Target | Action Required If Below Target |
|-------------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| Q1 (Mar 31) | ≥70% | Alert - monitor closely |
| Q2 (Jun 30) | ≥70% | Alert - monitor closely |
| Q3 (Sep 30) | ≥70% | Alert - monitor closely |
| Q4 (Dec 31) | ≥70% | Alert - monitor closely |

**If active % falls below 60%:** **URGENT** purification required.

**If active % falls below 55%:** **CRITICAL** - QSBC status in jeopardy.

### 13.2 Shareholder Agreement Provisions

**The Trust's ownership of Class B shares must be coordinated with corporate shareholder agreement.**

**Shareholder agreement should include:**

**(a) Drag-Along Rights:**

If founder sells Class A shares, buyer may require Trust to sell Class B shares on same terms (proportionate valuation).

**Why necessary:** Buyers typically want 100% ownership, not partial interest. Drag-along ensures Trust cannot block sale.

**(b) Tag-Along Rights:**

If founder sells Class A shares, Trust has right (but not obligation) to sell Class B shares on same terms (proportionate valuation).

**Why necessary:** Protects Trust from being left as minority shareholder in Corporation controlled by unknown third party.

**(c) Pro-Rata Participation:**

Trust has right to participate pro-rata in any liquidity event (minority sale, dividend distribution, redemption).

**Why necessary:** Ensures Trust benefits proportionately from any Corporation liquidity.

**(d) Founder Buy-Back Right:**

If Trust wishes to sell Class B shares, founder has first right of refusal to purchase at fair market value.

**Why necessary:** Keeps ownership within family, prevents third-party shareholders.

**(e) Voting Coordination:**

Even though Class B shares are non-voting, shareholder agreement should address:
- Information rights (Trust entitled to financial statements, strategic updates)
- Consultation on major decisions (M&A, financing, strategic pivots)
- Dispute resolution if founder and Trustees disagree

### 13.3 Estate Freeze Mechanics

**(a) Pre-Freeze State:**
- Founder owns 100% of Corporation common shares
- Shares have low fair market value (incorporation or early stage)

- Founder exchanges all common shares for preferred shares
- Preferred shares have fixed redemption value equal to current FMV (e.g., $1,000-10,000)
- Trust subscribes for new Class B common shares (400 shares at nominal value, e.g., $0.01/share = $4)

**(c) Post-Freeze State:**
- Founder holds preferred shares (fixed value, frozen at current FMV)
- Trust holds Class B common shares (nominal current value, unlimited future upside)
- **All future growth accrues to Trust's Class B shares**

**(d) LCGE Benefit:**
- Corporation grows from $10K FMV to $10M FMV (typical startup trajectory)
- Founder's preferred shares: Still worth $10K (frozen)
- Trust's Class B shares: Now worth $10M (all growth)
