# Technical Frameworks for CCPC and Family Trust Tax Planning

This reference provides detailed technical knowledge for structuring Canadian Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs) with integrated family trusts to maximize tax efficiency for tech company owners.

## The Core Tax Benefits: Why This Matters

### Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)
**Current limit (2025):** $1,254,000 per individual (indexed annually)
**Key benefit:** Tax-free realization of capital gains on Qualified Small Business Corporation (QSBC) shares

**Multiplication strategy:** Each family member with QSBC shares can claim their own LCGE
- Founder: $1.25M
- Spouse: $1.25M  
- Adult child 1: $1.25M
- Adult child 2: $1.25M
- **Total family:** $5M+ tax-free vs. $1.25M single-owner

**Tax savings calculation:**
- $5M LCGE vs. $1.25M = $3.75M additional exemption
- At 27% capital gains inclusion rate (first $250K) and 37% (over $250K): ~$1.1M in tax savings
- For tech company exits >$10M: savings can exceed $2M

### Small Business Deduction (SBD)
**Rate:** 9% federal corporate tax (vs. 15% general rate) on first $500K of active business income
**Additional benefit:** Combined federal-provincial rate typically 11-13% (vs. 26-31% general corporate rate)
**Annual tax savings:** ~$80K-100K per year for profitable companies

**CCPC preservation importance:** Losing CCPC status means losing SBD access and preferential dividend treatment

### Dividend Tax Integration
**Eligible dividends from CCPCs:** Lower personal tax rates on distributions
**Impact:** More tax-efficient income extraction for owner-managers vs. employment income

## QSBC Qualification Requirements (Critical)

All three tests must pass at time of sale for LCGE eligibility:

### 1. 24-Month Holding Period
**Rule:** Shares must be held by claimant for 24 months before sale
**Planning implication:** Estate freeze and family trust must occur ≥24 months before anticipated exit
**Common mistake:** Rushing estate freeze too close to sale discussions

**Example timeline:**
- Year 1: Series A funding, maintain CCPC status
- Year 2: Implement estate freeze with family trust (growth shares to beneficiaries)
- Years 3-4: Growth in value accrues to trust beneficiaries  
- Year 5: Exit - all beneficiaries meet 24-month test

### 2. 90% Active Business Assets Test
**Rule:** At time of sale, ≥90% of FMV of corporate assets must be used in active business
**Common violations:**
- Excess cash from profitable operations (>10% of total FMV)
- Investment portfolio (stocks, bonds)
- Rental properties not integral to business
- Passive income-generating assets

**Purification strategies:**
- Dividend excess cash to shareholders before sale
- Transfer investment assets to separate holding company
- Pay down debt with excess cash
- Reinvest in active business assets (equipment, inventory, R&D)

**Timing:** Purification must occur before share sale, allowing time to meet 90% test

### 3. 50% Active Business Assets Test (Historical)
**Rule:** Throughout any 24-month period before sale, >50% of FMV of assets must have been used in active business
**Difference from 90% test:** More lenient (50% vs. 90%), but applies historically over full 24 months
**Planning note:** Can hold some passive assets during growth phase, but must purify before sale

## CCPC Status Preservation Through Funding Rounds

### What Defines a CCPC?

**Three requirements (all must be met):**
1. **Canadian incorporation:** Must be incorporated under federal or provincial law
2. **Canadian control:** Must be controlled (>50% voting shares) by Canadian residents
3. **Not controlled by:** Public corporations, non-Canadian residents, or any combination thereof

### Foreign Investment Risks

**Most common CCPC status loss:** Accepting investment from non-Canadian VCs/strategic investors

**Control definition:** Both legal control (>50% voting) AND de facto control (operational control)
- Board composition matters
- Veto rights on major decisions
- Unanimous shareholder agreements

**Critical moment:** CCPC status lost "as soon as the purchase agreement is signed" (Taheri)

### Structuring Solutions for Foreign Investment

**Option 1: Non-voting preferred shares**
- Foreign investors receive preferred shares with economic rights but no voting rights
- Canadian founders retain 100% voting control
- Maintains CCPC status
- **Drawback:** Some VCs resist non-voting structures

**Option 2: Voting cap structures**
- Foreign investors get voting shares but capped at <50% total voting power
- Requires careful shareholder agreement drafting
- **Example:** Foreign VC gets 40% equity, 45% voting; founders retain 60% equity, 55% voting

**Option 3: Canadian holding company intermediary**
- Create Canadian-controlled holding company
- Holding company invests in operating company alongside foreign investors
- Operating company remains CCPC if Canadian holding company + founders maintain control
- More complex, higher setup costs

**Option 4: Parallel structure**
- Create US subsidiary for US operations/customers
- Keep Canadian parent as CCPC for Canadian revenue
- Transfer IP/assets strategically between entities
- Most complex, requires transfer pricing analysis

## Estate Freeze Fundamentals

### What Is an Estate Freeze?

**Purpose:** "Freeze" current value of business in preferred shares held by founder, while future growth accrues to common shares held by family trust/beneficiaries

**Tax objectives:**
1. Multiply LCGE benefits across family members
2. Minimize future estate taxes on founder's death
3. Enable income splitting through trust distributions (subject to TOSI)
4. Provide succession planning framework

### Basic Estate Freeze Mechanics

**Before freeze:**
- Founder owns 100 common shares, FMV $5M
- Company grows to $15M

**After freeze:**
- Founder owns preferred shares, redemption value frozen at $5M
- Family trust owns new common shares
- Company grows to $15M
- Trust (on behalf of beneficiaries) owns $10M growth
- Each beneficiary can claim LCGE on their portion

### Section 86 Share Exchange (Most Common Method)

**Process:**
1. Founder exchanges common shares for preferred shares
2. Preferred shares: Fixed redemption value = current FMV ($5M)
3. New common shares issued to family trust
4. Future growth accrues to new common shares
5. Tax-deferred exchange (no immediate tax)

**Preferred share terms:**
- Non-voting (usually)
- Fixed redemption value
- Retractable (founder can force redemption)
- Dividend rights (for income if needed)

**Key advantage:** No immediate tax on exchange (rollover at cost)

### Section 85 Rollover (Alternative Method)

**When used:** More complex scenarios, transferring multiple asset types, or specific elected amount needed

**Process:**
1. Founder transfers shares to holding company
2. Receives "boot" (cash/debt) up to tax cost + preferred shares
3. Files election to defer capital gains
4. More flexibility than Section 86 but more documentation

### Family Trust Structure

**Discretionary family trust typical structure:**
- Settlor: Third party (not founder - avoids attribution rules)
- Trustees: Founder and/or spouse (control distributions)
- Beneficiaries: Founder, spouse, children, grandchildren (including unborn)

**Discretion benefits:**
- Trustees decide which beneficiaries receive distributions each year
- Optimize for tax brackets
- Adapt to changing family circumstances
- Protect against creditors (trust property, properly structured)

### Unborn Children as Trust Beneficiaries - Critical Planning Point

**The question:** Can you include future/unborn children in a family trust without waiting for birth?

**Answer: YES** - Use class-based beneficiary language rather than naming individuals.

**Standard drafting language:**
- "The children of [Parent Names], whether now living or born hereafter"
- "All children, including those born or adopted after the date of this Trust"
- "The issue of [Parent Names], whether in being at the date hereof or born thereafter"

**Three critical advantages:**

1. **Automatic inclusion upon birth:** Future children become beneficiaries automatically without trust amendment
2. **Avoids subsection 75(2) attribution:** If settlor retains power to add beneficiaries later, can trigger attribution
3. **Starts 24-month holding period immediately:** No delay waiting for birth - critical for LCGE eligibility

**CRA administrative position:**
- Schedule 15 (T3 trust reporting) explicitly allows class descriptions when beneficiary identity "is not known or ascertainable with reasonable effort (for example, unborn children and grandchildren)"
- 2023 STEP/CRA Roundtable confirmed this approach

**CRITICAL RISK of naming only existing children:**
- If you name existing children only and later add unborn child → potential subsection 75(2) trigger
- Subsection 107(4.1) prevents tax-deferred distribution if 75(2) applies → defeats LCGE multiplication
- "Fix" requires trust resettlement (new trust, fresh 21-year clock, restart 24-month period)

**Example scenario:**
- Family expecting 3rd child in 4 months
- Wrong approach: Create trust naming 2 existing children, amend after birth
- Right approach: Create trust with class language including "children whether now living or born hereafter"
- Result: 3rd child automatically included, no amendment needed, 24-month clock starts 4 months earlier

### 21-Year Trust Rule - Advanced Planning

**The deemed disposition rule:**
- Family trusts face deemed disposition at FMV on 21st anniversary
- All accrued capital gains must be recognized and taxed
- Can result in significant tax bill without planning

**Planning strategies:**

**1. Section 107(2) rollover (primary strategy):**
- Distribute trust property to Canadian-resident beneficiaries BEFORE 21st anniversary
- Property transfers at adjusted cost base (ACB) with no immediate tax
- **CRITICAL LIMITATION:** Rollover unavailable if subsection 75(2) EVER applied to trust property
- Another reason to draft beneficiary language correctly from inception

**2. Indefeasible vesting:**
- Trusts with all interests "vested indefeasibly" exempt from 21-year rule
- Trust deed should grant trustees power to vest interests indefeasibly
- Provides flexibility to avoid deemed disposition

**3. Strategic distribution timing:**
- Year 20: Distribute appreciated property to beneficiaries via 107(2)
- Beneficiaries hold property with inherited ACB and holding period
- When beneficiaries sell, they claim their individual LCGEs

**Planning timeline example:**
- Trust created 2026
- 21st anniversary: 2047
- Child born 2026 would be ~21 years old in 2047
- Plan distributions in 2045-2046 (years 19-20) to maximize tax deferral
- Distribute to adult children who can claim LCGE on future sale

**Ongoing monitoring:**
- Annual trustee meeting minutes documenting distribution decisions
- Track each beneficiary's ACB and holding period
- Model tax impact of distributions vs. holding
- Review strategy every 5 years as family circumstances change

## Multi-Product Development Strategy and QSBC Qualification

### Does developing multiple products jeopardize QSBC status?

**Short answer: NO** - provided all products are part of active business operations.

For software/AI companies, the following qualify as active business assets:
- Developed software and IP (including in-development code)
- R&D expenditures and capitalized development costs
- Computer equipment and cloud computing costs
- Training data assets and ML models
- Working capital necessary for operations

**Product failures and pivots are normal:**
- Failed R&D remains "used in active business"
- Pivoting from one product to another doesn't retroactively disqualify
- Multiple simultaneous product development tracks = multiple R&D projects

**The critical distinction:**
- Active: Developing 5 AI products, 2 fail, 3 succeed → all development was active business
- Passive: One product succeeds, generates cash that sits idle while others remain pre-revenue

### The real QSBC risk: cash accumulation from one successful product

**When cash becomes passive:**
CRA treats cash as active asset ONLY if "its withdrawal would destabilize the business"

**Cash that qualifies as active:**
- 3-6 months operating expenses (payroll, rent, hosting)
- Amounts earmarked for specific near-term capital expenditures
- Tax installment obligations
- Amounts needed to maintain credit facilities

**Cash that becomes passive:**
- "Financial cushion" beyond working capital needs
- Cash accumulated for future "maybe" purchases
- Investment portfolio of stocks/bonds
- GICs or term deposits
- Excess cash without documented business purpose

**Example scenario:**
- OnDevice AI launches, generates $2M revenue
- Operating costs: $500K/year
- Company accumulates $1.5M cash
- Interior design AI and other products remain pre-revenue
- **QSBC risk:** If total assets = $2M, and $1.5M is excess cash, you fail the 50%/90% tests

### Mitigation strategies for multi-product companies

**1. Reinvest profits aggressively:**
- Hire developers for pre-revenue products
- Increase R&D expenditures
- Purchase training data or cloud computing credits
- Capital expenditures (servers, equipment)
- Patent/trademark filings

**2. Dividend to holding company:**
- Before year-end, dividend excess cash to HoldCo
- OpCo maintains lean balance sheet (passes 90% test)
- HoldCo holds passive assets (doesn't need to be QSBC)
- When selling OpCo, only OpCo needs to qualify

**3. Pay down debt:**
- Repay shareholder loans
- Pay off credit facilities
- Bring tax installments current

**4. Quarterly asset composition monitoring:**
- Calculate FMV of all assets quarterly
- Track active vs. passive percentages
- Set alerts at 65% active (buffer before 50% threshold)
- Document business purpose for all cash holdings

**5. Pre-sale purification:**
- 6-12 months before anticipated exit
- Dividend out all passive assets
- Ensure 90%+ active asset ratio at closing
- Note: Can only purify the "at sale" test (90%), not the historical test (50%)

### Single corporation vs. multiple OpCos under HoldCo

**For early-stage multi-product companies: Single OpCo is recommended**

| Single Corporation | HoldCo + Multiple OpCos |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| ✅ Simpler administration | ❌ Complex connected corporation rules |
| ✅ Lower costs ($2K-5K annual) | ❌ Multiple filings ($3K-8K per subsidiary) |
| ✅ Single SR&ED claim | ❌ Must allocate shared expenses |
| ✅ Clear QSBC qualification | ❌ Each OpCo must independently qualify |
| ✅ One set of books | ❌ Intercompany transactions require documentation |
| ✅ No connected corporation risks | ❌ One subsidiary failure can cascade |

**When HoldCo structure makes sense:**
- One product approaching significant revenue ($500K+)
- M&A interest in specific product (want to sell one, keep others)
- Products have distinctly different risk profiles
- Planning exit in 3+ years and want LCGE multiplication flexibility
- Foreign investor wants shares in specific product only

**Connected corporation QSBC rules:**
If you create HoldCo structure:
- Each OpCo must independently meet 50% active asset test throughout 24 months
- If ANY OpCo fails the test, it can disqualify HoldCo shares from LCGE
- Connected corporation = corporation connected with taxpayer or with another connected corporation
- All assets of connected corporations are aggregated for the 90% "at sale" test

**Practical example:**
- HoldCo owns 100% of OpCo A (OnDevice AI) and OpCo B (Interior Design AI)
- OpCo A succeeds, accumulates $2M cash
- OpCo B remains pre-revenue
- If OpCo A fails 50% test at any point → HoldCo shares disqualified for LCGE
- Better: Dividend $1.5M from OpCo A to HoldCo before failing 50% test

## Critical Tax Traps and How to Avoid Them

### Subsection 75(2) Attribution Rules

**The trap:** If settlor retains any reversionary right to trust property, all income/gains attributed back to settlor - eliminating tax benefits

**Triggering conditions (any one triggers attribution):**
1. Property can revert to settlor (now or in future)
2. Property passes to persons determined by settlor
3. Property cannot be disposed of without settlor's consent

**Safe harbor: *Sommerer* case (2011 TCC, 2012 FCA)**
- 75(2) doesn't apply to property sold to trust for fair market value consideration
- Trust must pay actual fair consideration (promissory note at market interest)
- No reversionary interest retained

**Defensive structure:**
- Third-party settlor (not founder)
- Irrevocable trust
- No settlor veto over trust decisions
- If transferring property to trust (vs. subscribing for new shares), use 85 rollover with promissory note

### Section 84.1 Surplus Stripping Rules

**The trap:** Individual sells shares to corporation they control → capital gain recharacterized as dividend (higher tax)

**When it applies:**
1. Individual resident in Canada
2. Sells shares of Canadian corporation
3. To another Canadian corporation
4. Both corporations non-arm's length
5. Seller owns ≥25% of buyer (directly or indirectly)

**Purpose:** Prevent extracting retained earnings as capital gains instead of dividends

**Common scenario:**
- Founder owns OpCo shares (cost base $100, FMV $5M)
- Sells to new HoldCo (founder controls)
- Without 84.1: $4.9M capital gain (tax-favored)
- With 84.1: Recharacterized as $4.9M deemed dividend (higher tax)

**Planning solutions:**
- Ensure transactions are at arm's length (properly structured trusts)
- Use 85 rollover instead of sale (different tax treatment)
- Structure multi-step reorganizations with holding companies
- Document non-tax business purposes

**Key case: *McMullen* (2018 FCA)**
- Confirmed no "overariding principle" requiring dividends
- But structure must have genuine arm's length characteristics
- Court examines substance over form

### TOSI (Tax on Split Income) - Post-2017 Complexity

**The problem:** 2017 tax reform targets income splitting with family members through private companies

**Highest marginal rate applied to:**
- Dividends from private corporation to adult family members
- Unless "excluded amount" exception applies

**Key exceptions for adult beneficiaries (18+):**
1. **Reasonable return for labor:** Adult contributed ≥20 hours/week (averaged over year or any prior 5 consecutive years)
2. **Reasonable return for capital:** 
   - Adult owns ≥10% votes + value AND
   - <90% of business income from services AND
   - Corporation not professional services (e.g., consulting)
3. **Inherited shares:** From deceased parent
4. **Age 25+:** More relaxed tests

**Planning implications:**
- Document children's contributions to business (hours, responsibilities)
- Consider waiting until children 25+ for easier qualification
- Structure operational business to minimize services revenue percentage
- Ensure trust distributions align with TOSI exceptions

**Common structure:**
- Children work in business during university/early career (building hours)
- Document 20+ hours/week over qualifying periods
- Future trust distributions qualify as "reasonable return for labor"

### GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rule)

**The principle:** Tax authorities can recharacterize transactions that are technically legal but constitute "abusive tax avoidance"

**Three-part test:**
1. Tax benefit obtained
2. Transaction is avoidance transaction (no bona fide non-tax purpose)
3. Abusive tax avoidance (frustrates object/spirit/purpose of provisions)

**Recent evolution:**

***DAC Investment Holdings* (2024 TCC) - Taxpayer win:**
- Continuance from Alberta to Ontario to exit CCPC status before realizing gains
- Court: Moving between tax regimes not inherently abusive
- Established legitimacy of jurisdiction selection

***Deans Knight* (2023 SCC) - CRA win:**
- Loss trading case
- Introduced "functional equivalence" test
- Transactions functionally equivalent to prohibited transactions may be abusive

**Defensive planning:**
1. **Document non-tax business purposes** for every significant step
   - Succession planning
   - Asset protection
   - Estate planning
   - Simplifying corporate structure
   - Facilitating future transactions

2. **Avoid purely tax-motivated timing** without substance
   - If restructuring immediately before exit announcement, create reasonable timeline
   - Implement estate freeze during business growth, not in response to buyer interest

3. **Maintain commercial substance**
   - Real trust operations (trustee meetings, documented decisions)
   - Arm's length dealings where required
   - Market-rate interest on promissory notes
   - Actual dividend distributions (not just paper allocations)

4. **Engage experienced counsel** for opinion letters
   - Documents reasonable position
   - Useful if CRA challenges structure
   - Shows good faith

## Implementation Sequence for Tech Companies

### Stage 1: Incorporation and Initial Structure (Pre-revenue to Seed)

**Timing:** At incorporation or as soon as practical

**Steps:**
1. **Incorporate as CCPC**
   - Federal incorporation (Business Corporations Act) or provincial
   - Ensure Canadian resident majority of directors
   - Standard articles enabling share class creation

2. **Create flexible share class structure**
   - Multiple classes of common shares (Class A, B, C, etc.)
   - Preferred share provisions (for future estate freeze)
   - Voting vs. non-voting designations
   - Different rights for different shareholders

3. **Shareholders agreement**
   - Buy-sell provisions
   - Drag-along/tag-along rights (for future investors)
   - Shotgun clauses
   - First right of refusal
   - Dispute resolution

4. **Maintain CCPC status**
   - Track shareholder residency
   - Ensure >50% Canadian voting control
   - Document board composition

**Legal counsel:** Boutique specialist (Rotfleisch) or major firm emerging companies practice (Osler)
**Cost:** $5,000-15,000

### Stage 2: Pre-Series A / Early Growth (Revenue <$2M)

**Timing:** 12-24 months before anticipated Series A

**Steps:**
1. **QSBC qualification audit**
   - Verify 90% active business assets
   - Clean up passive investments
   - Ensure Canadian control maintained

2. **IP and asset cleanup**
   - Transfer non-essential assets to personal name or holding company
   - Ensure operating company assets qualify as "active business"
   - Document use of all major assets

3. **Consider preliminary estate freeze IF:**
   - Valuation relatively low (<$2M)
   - ≥2 years before anticipated exit
   - Family members available as beneficiaries

**Why wait vs. doing immediately?**
- Lower initial valuation = less preferred share value frozen
- But need 24-month holding period for LCGE
- Balance: freeze when value is low but time allows 24-month period

**Legal counsel:** Boutique specialist or major firm
**Cost:** $10,000-25,000

### Stage 3: Series A Funding (Foreign or Public Company Investors)

**Timing:** During term sheet negotiations

**Critical considerations:**
1. **CCPC status preservation**
   - Review term sheet for control provisions
   - Negotiate voting structure
   - Consider non-voting preferred shares for foreign investors

2. **Employee stock options**
   - CCPC stock option deduction available (better than public company)
   - Align strike price with current FMV
   - Consider trust as optionee for key family members (complex, requires careful planning)

3. **Shareholder agreement updates**
   - Integrate new investor rights
   - Protect CCPC status in future rounds
   - Preserve founder control where possible

**Legal counsel:** Major firm with M&A + tax integration (Osler, Davies)
**Cost:** $20,000-50,000 (often rolled into overall financing legal costs)

### Stage 4: Post-Series A / Hypergrowth (Revenue $2M-20M)

**Timing:** 24-36 months before anticipated exit

**Steps:**
1. **Estate freeze with family trust (if not already done)**
   - Current valuation assessment
   - Section 86 share exchange
   - Create discretionary family trust
   - Issue new common shares to trust
   - File all required elections

2. **Trust structure optimization**
   - Identify all potential beneficiaries
   - Consider multiple trusts (one per child vs. one family trust)
   - Document trustee decision-making process

3. **TOSI planning**
   - Document family member contributions
   - Plan for reasonable return justifications
   - Consider timing of distributions

4. **Ongoing QSBC compliance**
   - Quarterly asset composition review
   - Proactive purification of passive assets
   - Monitor connected corporation rules

**Legal counsel:** Estate freeze specialist (Prasad, Louis, Weigl) or major firm private client (Davies, Osler)
**Cost:** $30,000-70,000

### Stage 5: Pre-Exit Optimization (Deal discussions active)

**Timing:** 6-12 months before anticipated closing

**Steps:**
1. **QSBC qualification verification**
   - Detailed asset-by-asset analysis
   - Purification of passive assets
   - Timing optimization (ensure 90% test passes at closing)

2. **Holding period confirmation**
   - Verify all beneficiaries meet 24-month test
   - Consider crystallization if some beneficiaries don't qualify yet

3. **Structure defensibility review**
   - Third-party legal opinion on QSBC qualification
   - GAAR analysis
   - Document business purposes for all prior reorganizations

4. **Exit structure planning**
   - Share sale vs. asset sale (QSBC requires share sale)
   - Purchase price allocation
   - Earnout and escrow implications for LCGE
   - Post-closing reorganization plans

**Legal counsel:** Elite boutique litigator (Thorsteinssons - Williams, Felesky Flynn - Fuller) for structure review
**Cost:** $30,000-100,000+ (depends on complexity and transaction size)

### Stage 6: Post-Exit Tax Optimization

**Steps:**
1. **LCGE claims**
   - Each beneficiary files claiming exemption
   - Proper documentation and elections
   - Capital gains reserve if applicable

2. **Trust distributions**
   - Distribute cash to beneficiaries tax-efficiently
   - Consider timing across tax years
   - Allocate to optimize family unit tax brackets

3. **Surplus extraction from preferreds**
   - Plan redemption of preferred shares held by founder
   - Optimize dividend timing and amounts
   - Consider capital gains strips if applicable

4. **Post-mortem planning**
   - Update wills and estate plans
   - Consider pipeline planning for death
   - Review insurance needs

## Remediation: What Happens When Initial Setup Is Wrong

### Available reorganization tools

**Section 86 reorganization (preferred for share structure fixes):**
- Tax-deferred exchange of old shares for new shares
- "Reorganization of capital" within same corporation
- **Key advantage:** No CRA election form required (simpler documentation)
- **Critical benefit:** New shares inherit holding period of old shares under substituted property rules
- **Process:** 4-8 weeks
- **Cost:** $15,000-$40,000 (legal, accounting, valuation)

**Section 85 rollover (for inserting holding companies):**
- Transfer shares to new holding company
- Requires Form T2057 joint election with CRA
- **Risk:** Section 84.1 anti-avoidance can apply
- When individuals transfer shares to corporation they control, 84.1 can:
  - Grind down paid-up capital
  - Convert capital gains into deemed dividends
  - Potentially deny LCGE benefits entirely
- **Cost:** $20,000-$50,000 (more complex than Section 86)

**"Wind up and restart" (almost never recommended):**
- Winding up triggers deemed disposition at FMV
- Immediate tax liability on all corporate assets
- Loss of all tax attributes
- **CRITICAL:** Restarts 24-month QSBC holding period
- Only viable if corporation has minimal value

### LCGE eligibility preservation during reorganization

**The 24-month QSBC tests:**
- At sale: 90%+ of asset FMV used in active business
- Throughout 24 months: >50% of asset FMV used in active business
- Ownership: Held by individual or related party for 24 months

**What can be fixed:**
- **90% "at sale" test** → Can purify immediately before sale (dividend out passive assets)
- **Section 86 shares** → Inherit original holding period via ITA 110.6(1)(e) substituted property rules

**What CANNOT be fixed:**
- **50% historical test** → If corporation failed at ANY point during 24-month lookback, no reorganization can cure it
- Must purify immediately and wait FULL 24 months from compliance date
- Past failures are permanent disqualifiers

**Example of unfixable situation:**
- Year 1: Corporation has $1M assets (60% active, 40% passive) ✅ Passes 50% test
- Year 2: Profitable, accumulates cash - $2M assets (30% active, 70% passive) ❌ Fails 50% test
- Year 3: Attempt to sell for $5M
- **Result:** Even if you purify to 95% active before sale (passes 90% test), the Year 2 failure permanently disqualifies the shares
- **Solution:** Purify to >50% active, then wait 24 months before any sale

### CRA challenges to late-stage reorganizations

**Key case: *D'Arcy v. The King* (2025 TCC 128)**
- GAAR applied to reorganization with "no bona fide commercial justification apart from" tax benefit
- Court scrutinized timing: reorganization shortly before sale discussions

**CRA's stated targeting (2023 CTF Conference):**
- "Last-minute purifications" within 6 months of exit
- Aggressive LCGE multiplication attempts
- Reorganizations without documented business purposes

**Protective measures:**
1. **Document business purposes for ALL reorganizations:**
   - Succession planning
   - Asset protection
   - Estate planning simplification
   - Facilitating future transactions
   - Family governance structures
   
2. **Implement changes 24+ months before anticipated sale:**
   - Creates timeline buffer
   - Demonstrates long-term planning, not tax avoidance
   - Allows beneficiaries to meet holding period

3. **Obtain professional valuations:**
   - Support all transfer prices
   - Document FMV at time of reorganization
   - Required for Section 85/86 elections

4. **Consider advance tax ruling if LCGE benefit exceeds $500K:**
   - CRA provides binding ruling on structure
   - Costs $5,000-$20,000 but provides certainty
   - Takes 3-6 months to obtain

### Cost comparison: proper setup vs. remediation

| Scenario | Cost | Timeline | Risk |
|----------|------|----------|------|
| **Proper initial structure** | $12,000-$20,000 | 6-8 weeks | Minimal - 24-month clock starts immediately |
| **Basic incorporation, fix with Section 86** | $20,000-$45,000 | 12-16 weeks | Medium - May delay 24-month clock |
| **Basic incorporation, fix with Section 85 + HoldCo** | $25,000-$65,000 | 16-24 weeks | High - Section 84.1 risk, complex |
| **Wind up and restart** | Tax + $15,000+ | 8-12 weeks | Very high - Restarts 24-month clock entirely |

**Key insight:** Remediation costs 1.5x to 3x more than proper initial setup, with added timeline delays and qualification risks.

## OBCA vs. CBCA Incorporation for Tech Companies

### Recommendation: OBCA (Ontario Business Corporations Act)

**Three decisive factors for tech companies:**

**1. Director residency requirements:**
- **OBCA:** ZERO Canadian resident director requirement (eliminated July 5, 2021 via Bill 213)
- **CBCA:** 25% of directors must be Canadian residents
- **Impact:** OBCA provides flexibility if seeking foreign VC investment or hiring international executives to board

**2. Administrative costs and complexity:**
- **OBCA:** 
  - Annual return fee: $0
  - Single filing with Ontario
  - No federal filings
- **CBCA:**
  - Annual return to Corporations Canada
  - Must ALSO file Ontario Initial Return/Notice of Change
  - Individuals with Significant Control (ISC) reporting annually
  - **Total cost difference:** ~$300-$600/year higher for CBCA

**3. Tax treatment (identical):**
- CCPC status depends on control, not incorporation jurisdiction
- OITC (Ontario Innovation Tax Credit) available to both
- SR&ED credits available to both
- Small Business Deduction available to both
- **No tax advantage to either choice**

### Choose CBCA if:

- **National expansion planned:** Automatic right to operate Canada-wide without extra-provincial registrations
- **National name protection:** CBCA name is protected nationally; OBCA only in Ontario
- **International investor preference:** Some foreign investors prefer "federal" incorporation for perceived prestige
- **Multi-provincial operations from day one:** Saves future extra-provincial registration costs

### Choose OBCA if (OnDevice AI's situation):

- **Ontario-focused operations:** Primary customers/operations in Ontario
- **Seeking foreign VC investment:** No director residency requirements
- **Cost minimization:** Lower ongoing costs
- **Simplicity:** Single jurisdiction reporting

**Practical note:** Can convert from OBCA to CBCA later via continuance (cost: $2,000-$5,000), but rarely necessary.

## Ontario-Specific Tax Rates and Credits

### Combined corporate tax rates (2025)

| Income Type | Rate | Details |
|------------|------|---------|
| **Small business income** (≤$500K active) | **12.2%** | 3.2% Ontario + 9% federal |
| **General corporate income** (>$500K) | **26.5%** | 11.5% Ontario + 15% federal |
| **Investment income** | **50.2%** | 11.5% Ontario + 38.7% federal (refundable) |

### Innovation tax credits for tech companies

**Ontario Innovation Tax Credit (OITC):**
- **8% refundable credit** on first $3M qualifying R&D expenditures
- Eligibility: Ontario CCPCs with permanent establishment in Ontario
- Qualifying expenses: Salaries, materials, overhead, contracts
- Claim via Schedule 566 (Ontario T2)

**SR&ED (Scientific Research & Experimental Development):**
- **Federal: 35% refundable** on first $3M (CCPCs)
- **Federal: 15% non-refundable** on amounts over $3M
- **Combined with OITC: ~46.5% of R&D costs** recovered

**Practical example for OnDevice AI:**
- R&D expenditures: $500K (developer salaries, cloud computing, training data)
- OITC credit: $500K × 8% = $40,000 refundable
- SR&ED federal credit: $500K × 35% = $175,000 refundable
- **Total credits: $215,000** (43% of R&D costs recovered)

### Ontario passive investment income advantage

**Critical difference from federal rules:**
- Federal: Passive income $50K-$150K **grinds down** Small Business Deduction
- Ontario: **NO PARALLEL GRIND** - Ontario SBD not affected by passive income
- **Practical impact:** If your CCPC accumulates investment income, Ontario rate advantage partially preserved

### Digital Media Tax Credit (DMTC) - potentially relevant

For interactive digital media products (games, educational software, interactive apps):
- 40% refundable tax credit on eligible Ontario labour expenditures
- Products must be developed primarily for entertainment or educational purposes
- Likely NOT applicable to OnDevice AI (utility tool), but potentially applicable to other products depending on design
