# Canada's elite CRA audit teams targeting tech companies using family trusts

**The specialized divisions and audit programs that generate billions in reassessments annually by scrutinizing CCPC + family trust structures.** For tech founders using family trusts to multiply the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption, understanding which CRA teams conduct these audits, their methodologies, and their track record can inform better tax planning and audit preparedness. This analysis identifies the key CRA divisions, programs, and enforcement statistics based on departmental reports, audit statistics, and documented enforcement outcomes.

The CRA's audit landscape divides into specialized programs: the Global High Wealth (GHW) audit program (formerly Related Party Audit Program), the High Complexity Audit Tax Services Office, the Aggressive Tax Planning division, and the International and Large Business Directorate. Each targets different risk profiles with varying audit methodologies and success rates.

---

## Key CRA audit divisions targeting tech company trust structures

**High Complexity Audit Tax Services Office (TSO)** operates as the CRA's specialized unit for auditing the most sophisticated tax planning arrangements. Headquartered in **Surrey, British Columbia** at the King George Boulevard campus, this office serves as the hub for complex audit files across Canada despite its Western Region origins. The mandate centers on aggressive tax planning schemes, intricate transactions, and structures designed to minimize tax—precisely the profile of tech companies using CCPC + family trust structures.

The High Complexity TSO received substantial funding increases: **Budget 2022 provided $1.2 billion over five years** specifically to expand audits of larger entities and non-residents engaged in aggressive tax planning. This investment was projected to recover approximately **$3.4 billion in additional revenue** over five years. The Fall Economic Statement 2020 committed resources for over 600 new field auditors dedicated to high-complexity cases.

**Global High Wealth (GHW) Audit Program** represents the CRA's most comprehensive approach to auditing wealthy individuals and their associated entities. The program has **more than doubled its identified target population** from approximately 800 groups in 2018 to **over 2,500 groups** as of 2024. The CRA defines GHW groups as "identified groups of high-net-worth individuals, associates and related entities" rather than single taxpayers.

**Current audit scale**: The CRA has identified more than 2,500 GHW groups qualifying for audit, with over **600 individual audits currently in progress**. During April 2014 to September 2019, more than **900 GHW audits were completed**. In fiscal 2023-24 alone, the GHW program completed approximately **180 individual audits** generating a total fiscal impact of **$745 million**—representing 41% of the $1.8 billion total fiscal impact from all high-net-worth enforcement programs combined.

The GHW program formerly required individuals to have 25+ related-party entities and $50M+ net worth. The CRA **removed the 25-entity threshold** in recent years, dramatically expanding the program's scope. Each audit now examines entire groups that "may contain hundreds of entities, including individuals, trusts, partnerships and corporations."

**Audit team structure**: The CRA has **more than 30 dedicated RPAP/GHW audit teams**, up from 13 teams before Budget 2016 funding increases. Multiple auditors are typically assigned to each file—not just one—reflecting the complexity of reviewing entire economic groups. Audits are "complex and lengthy to complete," often taking **several years** from initiation to resolution.

## Audit methodologies and techniques deployed against family trusts

**Net Worth Audit** serves as the "nuclear bomb" in CRA's audit arsenal according to tax practitioners. The methodology compares assets and liabilities at the beginning and end of an audit period, adds estimated expenses, and presumes the difference to be unreported income. The CRA employs this method when:
- Taxpayers have inadequate books and records
- Initial audit stages reveal serious discrepancies between spending habits and reported income
- The CRA suspects unreported income that cannot be identified through direct means

The formula: **Opening Net Worth + Reported Income – Expenditures = Closing Net Worth**. Any increase beyond reported income is presumed to be unreported income.

Subsection 152(7) of the Income Tax Act grants CRA auditors sweeping authority: they are **not bound by any information provided by the taxpayer** and may assess based on information obtained through their own means. Auditors make "reasonable assumptions" supportable by evidence, including industry averages from Statistics Canada when actual records are unavailable.

**Economic Entity Approach** examines interrelated businesses and individuals—such as a family trust and its beneficiaries—as **one economic unit** to uncover tax planning strategies that shift income. For tech companies, this means CRA auditors will examine:
- The CCPC operating company
- Holding corporations
- Family trust structures
- Individual family members' returns
- Related partnerships or joint ventures
- Any offshore entities controlled by the group

**Bank Deposit Analysis** reviews all bank statements and totals deposits. If $150,000 in deposits appear but only $90,000 in income was reported, CRA will investigate the $60,000 discrepancy. For tech companies with complex cash flows through multiple entities, this creates significant audit risk.

**Lifestyle Analysis** compares an individual's reported income against observable spending patterns. CRA auditors use third-party data including:
- Real estate transactions and ownership records
- Vehicle registrations and luxury purchases
- Credit card statements
- Travel expenditures
- Private school tuition
- Property tax assessments

The CRA's risk assessment models **detect anomalies like individuals reporting low personal income while controlling high-value assets through holding companies**—a common pattern for tech founders using CCPC + family trust structures.

## Specific audit triggers for CCPC + family trust structures

**Trust reporting failures** now represent a major audit trigger. Enhanced trust reporting rules enacted for tax years ending on or after December 31, 2023 require T3 Schedule 15 ("Beneficial ownership information of a trust") disclosing detailed information about trustees, beneficiaries, settlors, and protectors. The CRA received **52,000 trust returns filed for bare trusts for 2023** alone despite initial filing exemptions.

**Passive income thresholds** trigger scrutiny. CCPCs must monitor passive income to maintain Small Business Deduction eligibility. **Investment income exceeding $50,000** reduces SBD eligibility and raises the federal tax rate on passive income to **38.67%**. For tech companies holding excess cash or investment portfolios, this creates audit exposure.

**QSBC qualification issues** represent frequent audit targets. To qualify for the Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption, shares must meet:
- 24-month holding period requirement
- 50%/90% asset tests (percentage of assets used in active business)
- CCPC status at time of disposition

Tech companies frequently fail QSBC tests due to:
- Excess cash and investment holdings disqualifying the 50%/90% thresholds
- Intellectual property holding structures
- Foreign subsidiaries affecting CCPC status
- Passive income exceeding thresholds

**Subsection 75(2) attribution** scrutiny follows the *Sommerer* decision framework. While the Federal Court of Appeal narrowed the rule's application in 2012, CRA auditors remain vigilant about trust structures where:
- Property can revert to the settlor
- The settlor retains significant control over trust property
- The distinction between settlor contributions and trust subscriptions is unclear

**GAAR applications** by CRA auditors have expanded dramatically since the 2024 amendments (Bill C-59). The new provisions introduce:
- **25% penalty** on denied tax benefits
- "One of the main purposes" test (lower threshold than previous "primary purpose")
- Extended reassessment periods by three years
- $50,000 minimum threshold for GAAR penalty application

As of 2014, **54 GAAR cases had been litigated with courts upholding GAAR in 28 cases** (roughly 50% CRA success rate). However, recent legislative changes significantly favor CRA enforcement.

**TOSI (Tax on Split Income) audits** target income splitting arrangements with family members. The excluded shares exception requires shares to be owned **directly**—family trust ownership does not qualify. CRA auditors scrutinize dividend distributions to family members under 25 years old or those not actively engaged in the business.

## CRA's track record and success rates

**Objection outcomes** reveal that taxpayers achieve full or partial success in a significant portion of cases. A 2018 Auditor General Report found that **60% of taxpayer objections were fully or partially allowed**—meaning the audit result was overturned at the objection stage. This indicates CRA auditors frequently overreach or apply the law incorrectly in initial assessments.

**Current objection volumes** have soared from approximately **68,000 objections in 2018-19 to 128,000 in 2024-25**—nearly doubling in six years. The CRA attributes this increase to:
- Growing technical complexity of the Income Tax Act
- Increased audit activity following Budget 2016-2022 funding
- More aggressive enforcement stances

**Processing delays** are substantial. The CRA aims to resolve low-complexity objections within 180 days and medium-complexity disputes within one year, achieving this standard only **61% of the time for simple objections and 69% for medium-complexity ones** in 2023-24. Performance improved slightly to 76% and 71% respectively in 2024-25, but still falls short of the 80% target.

**Tax Court outcomes** demonstrate mixed CRA success. While specific court victory statistics are not consistently published, case law analysis shows:
- GAAR cases: approximately 50% CRA success rate historically
- Net worth audits: High initial reassessment amounts frequently reduced significantly on appeal
- Trust attribution: Taxpayer victories in *Sommerer*, *Brent Kern Family Trust*, and others
- Offshore trust residency: Major CRA victories like *Garron Family Trust* ($152M tax upheld)

**Gross reassessment totals** exceed **$12 billion annually** in recent years, with over 60% related to tax avoidance by large multinational corporations and aggressive tax planning by wealthy individuals. However, these figures represent CRA's proposed reassessments—not final collected amounts after objections and appeals.

## Advanced data analytics and risk assessment tools

**Business Intelligence and Risk Management Division** within the Compliance Programs Branch provides support to the International and Large Business Directorate. The division acquires and maintains sophisticated data analytics capabilities that:
- Parse large datasets including international fund transfers and corporate filings
- Flag high-risk structures using machine learning algorithms
- Detect anomalies in filing patterns
- Cross-reference domestic and international data sources

**International data sharing** provides CRA access to:
- Common Reporting Standard (CRS) information from **over 100 countries**
- Automatic exchange of financial account information covering millions of offshore transactions
- Joint Chiefs of Global Tax Enforcement (J5) intelligence on high-profile tax evasion
- OECD information sharing agreements with **over 90 jurisdictions**

**AI-driven risk analytics** allow CRA to:
- Adapt to new fraud schemes in real-time
- Apply machine learning to detect patterns missed by human auditors
- Identify circular money flows and artificial loss creation
- Match third-party data (employment, investment income, property transactions) against filed returns

For tech companies, this means CRA can now:
- Identify discrepancies between reported income and observable business success (funding rounds, revenue growth, employee counts)
- Cross-reference founder equity positions against CCPC shareholder records
- Detect trust structures designed primarily for tax benefits rather than asset protection
- Compare industry benchmarks against individual company filings

## Audit process timeline and taxpayer rights

**Initial contact** typically occurs through:
- Audit notification letter specifying taxation years under review
- T997 "Audit Query Sheet" (for GHW audits)
- Detailed 20-page questionnaire for related party structures
- 30-day response deadline from receipt

**Information requests** under section 231.1 of the Income Tax Act grant CRA authority to:
- Examine books, records, and documents
- Enter business premises
- Require taxpayers to answer questions in any form specified
- Compel third parties to provide information

**Budget 2024 expanded audit powers** (proposed, not yet fully enacted):
- **Notice of Non-Compliance**: $50/day penalty (up to $25,000) for non-cooperation
- **Sworn testimony**: Authority to require responses under oath or affirmation
- **Compliance order penalties**: Additional 10% of aggregate tax payable if Federal Court compliance order not followed

**Reassessment periods**:
- Normal reassessment period: **3 years** from initial assessment date for individuals, **4 years** for corporations
- Extended to **6 years** if GAAR applies (under proposed amendments)
- **No time limit** for cases involving fraud, misrepresentation, or gross negligence

**Privilege protections**:
- Communications with CPAs are **NOT protected** from CRA compulsion
- Lawyer-client communications **ARE protected**
- "Kovel arrangements" where CPAs work under lawyer direction can extend privilege

## Audit triggers specific to tech sector

**Funding round valuations** create audit exposure when:
- Personal returns report low income while company raises millions at high valuations
- Founder equity positions increase dramatically without corresponding income recognition
- Share exchanges or reorganizations occur around funding events
- CCPC status threatened by non-resident investor participation

**Stock option exercises** trigger scrutiny around:
- Timing relative to liquidity events or QSBC crystallization
- Whether employee vs. founder treatment applied correctly
- Capital gains vs. employment income characterization
- Trust ownership of option-bearing shares

**SR&ED credit claims** face intensive audit when:
- Credits claimed exceed typical industry benchmarks
- Eligible vs. ineligible expenditure boundaries unclear
- Documentation insufficient to support claimed activities
- Overlap between SR&ED activities and general business operations

**Intellectual property structures** raise flags when:
- IP transferred to holding companies at values below market
- Licensing arrangements between related entities appear artificial
- IP ownership separated from entity conducting R&D
- Transfer pricing on IP-related transactions not supportable

## Internal CRA committees and decision-making bodies

**GAAR Committee** operates as CRA's interdepartmental review body for GAAR applications. Between GAAR's inception (1988) and March 31, 2017, the provision was applied to **1,093 files**. The committee:
- Reviews proposed GAAR assessments before finalization
- Provides consistency across CRA regions
- Establishes internal precedents for GAAR application
- Coordinates with Department of Finance on policy implications

**Audit File Resolution Committee (AFRC)** considers proposed audit agreements and settlements for:
- Subjective and material issues (valuations, reasonableness of expenses)
- Large business and high-net-worth audit agreements
- Cases where early resolution serves public interest
- Situations where taxpayers waive objection rights for certainty

AFRC meets every **4-6 weeks** and evaluates:
- Overall merits of taxpayer's position vs. audit team's perspective
- Whether proposed resolution achieves fair outcome for both parties
- Policy implications and precedent-setting considerations
- Litigation risk and cost-benefit analysis

**Transfer Pricing Review Committee** handles complex international arrangements. For tech companies with foreign subsidiaries or parent companies, this committee reviews:
- Intercompany pricing on IP licenses, services, and goods
- Cost-sharing arrangements for R&D
- Management fee structures
- Profit allocation methodologies

## Conclusion: Understanding the adversary's capabilities

The CRA has fundamentally transformed its audit operations over the past decade through massive funding increases, advanced data analytics, international cooperation, and specialized team structures. Tech founders using CCPC + family trust structures face audit teams that:

- Examine entire economic groups, not individual entities
- Deploy sophisticated data analytics detecting discrepancies between reported income and observable success
- Access international financial information automatically from 100+ countries
- Apply net worth and lifestyle audits when direct evidence is unavailable
- Coordinate across multiple specialized divisions and committees

The statistics reveal the scale: **$1.8 billion in fiscal impact from 700 audits** in 2023-24, with **average GHW audit generating $4.1 million** in reassessments. While 60% of taxpayer objections succeed fully or partially, this means enduring multi-year disputes, substantial professional fees, and significant business disruption.

The highest-ROI defensive strategy remains **early engagement with specialized tax counsel** before structures are implemented. Once CRA auditors arrive with T997 questionnaires and sweeping information requests, options narrow substantially. Founders should establish relationships with tax lawyers experienced in CRA audit defense and ensure CCPC + family trust structures withstand scrutiny under the methodologies CRA now routinely deploys.

Understanding which CRA teams conduct these audits, their methodologies, success rates, and capabilities enables tech founders to make informed decisions about tax planning complexity, documentation requirements, and when aggressive structures cross the line into territory where the $4.1 million average reassessment becomes personally relevant.
