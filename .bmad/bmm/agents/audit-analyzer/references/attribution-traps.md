# Subsection 75(2) Attribution and Section 107(4.1) Traps

## Subsection 75(2) Overview

**Purpose:** Prevent property transfers to trusts while settlor retains control or reversionary rights

**Effect:** Income and capital gains attributed back to settlor/contributor

**Critical for LCGE Planning:** Attributed gains are NOT eligible for LCGE per CRA IT-369R

## The Three Triggering Conditions

Subsection 75(2) applies when ALL THREE conditions met:

### Condition 1: Property Held by Trust

**Any property transferred to trust by:**
- Settlor (person who established trust)
- Subsequent contributor
- Person related to settlor/contributor

**"Property" Includes:**
- CCPC shares
- Cash
- Real estate
- Any asset

**Substituted Property:**
75(2) applies to property substituted for original property
- Trust sells shares, buys real estate = 75(2) still applies
- Attribution follows the property through transformations

### Condition 2: Property Can Revert to Contributor

**Triggering Language:**
"Property...may...revert to the person from whom the property...was derived"

**Includes:**
- Express reversion clause in trust deed
- Power to revoke trust and return property
- Power to appoint contributor as beneficiary
- Any contingent right to receive property back

**Does NOT Include:**
- Contributor named as fixed beneficiary from inception (not "reversion")
- Contributor has beneficial interest ab initio (already owns it beneficially)

### Condition 3: Property May Vest in Persons Determined by Contributor

**Triggering Language:**
Property can "become payable to...persons to be determined by the person from whom the property was derived"

**Includes:**
- Power of appointment held by contributor
- Contributor can add/remove beneficiaries
- Contributor can change distribution percentages
- Any discretion over who receives property

**Key Case Law:**

***Sommerer* (2012 FCA):**
- Beneficiary sold property to trust at FMV
- NOT a "contributor" under 75(2)
- 75(2) applies only to settlors and subsequent contributors
- FMV sales do not trigger attribution

***Brent Kern* (2013 TCC):**
- Applied *Sommerer* principles
- Each property transfer analyzed separately
- Trust deed terms critical to analysis

***Howson* (2007 TCC):**
- Genuine loan (even interest-free) NOT subject to 75(2)
- Must be true debt obligation
- Not mere accommodation

## Safe Structures to Avoid 75(2)

### Structure 1: Trust Subscribes for Shares (Cleanest)

**Setup:**
1. Founder creates Family Trust (spouse as settlor with nominal $100)
2. Founder NOT named as settlor or beneficiary
3. Trust subscribes for common shares of NewCo at inception
4. Founder subscribes for different class (preferred freeze shares)

**Why Safe:**
- Trust is original owner of common shares (no "transfer")
- Property did not come "from" founder
- No reversion possible (founder never had beneficial interest)
- Beneficiaries fixed at inception (no contributor power to change)

**Win Probability: 90%+**

### Structure 2: Trust Purchases at Fair Market Value (*Sommerer*)

**Setup:**
1. Trust established with independent settlor
2. Trust purchases CCPC shares from founder at FMV
3. Trust pays cash or assumes debt at fair value
4. Independent valuation obtained

**Why Safe:**
- *Sommerer*: Beneficiary who sells at FMV is not "contributor"
- Property derived from arm's length purchase, not contribution
- Founder has no reversion rights post-sale

**Requirements:**
- Truly independent valuation
- Real consideration paid
- Arm's length terms
- No benefit conferred to founder

**Win Probability: 75-85%**

### Structure 3: Genuine Loan to Trust (*Howson*)

**Setup:**
1. Founder loans money to trust
2. Trust uses loan to purchase shares
3. Real debt obligation with repayment terms
4. Documented properly

**Why Safe:**
- Loan is genuine debt, not contribution
- Property (shares) acquired with loan proceeds
- Not property "from" lender

**Requirements:**
- Real loan documentation
- Repayment terms (even if flexible)
- Not mere accommodation
- Commercial substance

**Win Probability: 70-80%**

## Dangerous Structures (75(2) Applies)

### Red Flag 1: Settlor as Beneficiary

**Structure:**
- Founder establishes trust
- Founder named as discretionary beneficiary
- Trust acquires CCPC shares

**Problem:**
- Property CAN revert to settlor (Condition 2 met)
- Even if remote contingency
- Even if "in extremis" distribution clause

**CRA Position:**
- Any possibility of reversion triggers 75(2)
- Does not matter if probability is low
- Discretionary power is sufficient

**Win Probability if Challenged: 10-25%**

### Red Flag 2: Contributor Controls Beneficiary Appointments

**Structure:**
- Founder contributes property to trust
- Trust deed allows founder (or related person) to appoint new beneficiaries
- Or remove existing beneficiaries

**Problem:**
- Property can vest in persons determined by contributor (Condition 3 met)
- Power to appoint is sufficient even if never exercised

**Common Mistake:**
- Power of appointment in will
- Letter of wishes giving founder input
- Protector role held by founder

**Win Probability if Challenged: 15-30%**

### Red Flag 3: Gift of Property to Trust

**Structure:**
- Founder gifts CCPC shares to family trust
- No consideration received
- Beneficiaries are founder's children

**Problem:**
- Founder is "contributor"
- IF trust deed has any reversion or appointment powers, 75(2) applies
- Even standard trust language can trigger

**Win Probability Depends on Trust Deed:**
- Pristine deed, no reversion: 60-70%
- Standard template with contingencies: 30-40%
- Poor drafting: 10-20%

## Section 107(4.1): The Permanent Trap

**Critical Rule:** If 75(2) has applied to ANY property of a trust at ANY TIME, section 107(4.1) denies rollover treatment on distributions to beneficiaries

**Effect:**
- Trust distributes property to beneficiary
- Normally section 107(2) rollover applies (no tax)
- But 107(4.1) denies rollover if 75(2) ever applied
- Creates immediate taxable capital gain

**"Ever Applied" = Permanent Taint:**
- Even if property that triggered 75(2) later disposed of
- Even if attribution no longer occurs
- Once 75(2) applies, trust is permanently tainted for 107(4.1)

**Cannot Be Fixed Retroactively:**
- *Collins* (2022 SCC): No "undo" for tax mistakes
- If trust structure triggers 75(2), cannot be remedied
- Distribution will trigger tax regardless

**Planning Implication:**
- Trust MUST be structured correctly from inception
- Cannot fix 75(2) problems later
- Conservative approach essential

## Interaction with LCGE Planning

**Worst Case Scenario:**

1. Trust structured incorrectly (75(2) applies)
2. Trust holds CCPC shares that appreciate
3. Trust distributes shares to beneficiary to claim LCGE
4. Section 107(4.1) denies rollover
5. Trust pays tax on capital gain
6. Trust cannot claim LCGE (attribution income not eligible)
7. Beneficiary receives shares with tax cost = FMV at distribution
8. Beneficiary can claim LCGE on future gain
9. Result: Trust pays full tax, no LCGE benefit realized

**Tax Difference:**
- Proper structure: $0 tax on $5M gain (LCGE)
- Improper structure: ~$1.25M tax to trust + lost LCGE benefit

**Recovery Impossible:**
- *Collins* principle: Cannot rescind or remedy
- Tax paid is permanent
- LCGE opportunity lost

## Trustee Composition Red Flags

**High Risk:**
- Settlor/contributor as sole trustee
- Settlor/contributor as co-trustee with veto
- Related person as sole trustee with contributor direction

**Medium Risk:**
- Related person as trustee without clear independence
- Professional trustee but contributor involvement in decisions
- Trustee appointment/removal by contributor

**Low Risk:**
- Independent professional trustees
- Multiple trustees, contributor not among them
- Clear independence documented

## CRA Audit Approach to 75(2)

**What CRA Examines:**

1. **Trust Deed Analysis**
   - Read entire deed word-by-word
   - Look for any reversion language
   - Identify any appointment powers
   - Note settlor identity

2. **Property Source Tracing**
   - How did trust acquire property?
   - Who contributed property?
   - What consideration paid?
   - Related party transaction analysis

3. **Trustee Independence**
   - Who are trustees?
   - Relationship to settlor/contributor?
   - Who directs trustee actions?
   - Are decisions truly independent?

4. **Beneficiary Analysis**
   - Is settlor/contributor a beneficiary?
   - Can they become one?
   - Any "in extremis" clauses?
   - Distribution history

**CRA's Burden:**
Must prove 75(2) applies (all three conditions)

**Taxpayer's Burden:**
If 75(2) position challenged, must prove either:
- Not a contribution (was FMV sale, loan, or trust was original owner)
- No reversion possible
- No contributor control over beneficiaries

## Defensive Strategies

**At Trust Creation:**

1. **Use Independent Settlor**
   - NOT the founder
   - Spouse with nominal $100 contribution
   - Third-party settlor

2. **Exclude Founder as Beneficiary**
   - Absolutely no path for property to revert
   - Not even contingently or remotely
   - Remove all "in extremis" language

3. **Fixed Beneficiaries from Inception**
   - No power to add/remove/change
   - Distribution percentages set in deed
   - Zero discretion by contributor

4. **Independent Trustees**
   - Professional trustee services
   - Not related to contributor
   - Clear authority to act independently

5. **Property Acquisition Method**
   - BEST: Trust subscribes for shares at inception
   - GOOD: Trust purchases at true FMV with independent valuation
   - RISKY: Gift to trust (only if deed perfect)

**For Existing Trusts:**

1. **Review Trust Deed Immediately**
   - Identify any 75(2) triggers
   - If found, consider whether remediation possible
   - May need new trust structure

2. **Cannot Fix Retroactively**
   - *Collins*: No equitable rescission
   - If 75(2) applied, taint is permanent
   - Distribution will trigger 107(4.1)

3. **Possible Mitigations:**
   - Freeze current trust structure (stop new property additions)
   - Create new compliant trust for future planning
   - Prepare for tax on distribution from tainted trust
   - Consider crystallizing gains before distribution if beneficial

## Confidence Scoring

**Trust Structure Assessment:**

**75(2) Compliance: CLEAN (85-95% confidence)**
- Independent settlor, founder not beneficiary
- Trust subscribed for shares OR purchased at FMV
- No reversion clauses
- Fixed beneficiaries
- Independent trustees

**75(2) Compliance: MODERATE RISK (50-70% confidence)**
- Some concerning language in deed
- Related party as trustee
- Some discretionary powers
- FMV transaction but valuation soft

**75(2) Compliance: HIGH RISK (20-40% confidence)**
- Founder as settlor or beneficiary
- Contributor has appointment powers
- Gift to trust with poor deed
- No independent valuation

**75(2) Compliance: CERTAIN FAILURE (5-15% confidence)**
- Founder as settlor AND beneficiary
- Express reversion clause
- Contributor controls all decisions
- Accommodation transaction

**Impact on Overall LCGE Planning:**
- 75(2) applies = LCGE benefit lost entirely
- Even moderate risk should be remediated
- Conservative approach essential given no-undo rule
